Early life

Taras Shevchenko was born on February 25, 1814. He was the third child in a family of serfs living in the village of Moryntsi, Kyiv province. (Source)

Taras Shevchenko liked to visit his grandfather, who often told him about the Haidamaks, Zalizniak, or Honta. Those times when the peasant family was burning. Painting by Shevchenko. lord's houses, later expressed in Taras's dreams and his works. At the age of 8, Taras Tevchenko went to learn to read and write from the local clerk. At the same time, he was first introduced to the works of the Ukrainian philosopher Hryhorii Skovoroda. (Source)

In 1823, Taras's mother, Kateryna Shevchenko, died, and he had to leave school to take care of his younger children. When his father remarried (the same year), there were frequent quarrels in the house. The widowed stepmother treated other people's children badly because she had her own. Later, another sister, Mariia, was born from her father's second marriage. And soon (just a year later) Hryhorii Shevchenko became weak and died. The stepmother moved back to Morintsy. (Source)

Taras returned to school, where they sent a new deacon, Bohorsky. He was a very learned man, but a heavy drinker. He treated Taras extremely badly, the boy worked more for the deacon than he studied, but he was still often beaten. Unable to bear the abuse, young Shevchenko ran away from Bohorsky and went in search of a teacher of painting. For several days he stayed with teachers from the surrounding villages, to whom he was hired as an apprentice. At the same time, Taras met his first love, Oksana, a girl who was his friend and whom he often remembered fondly, including in his works. (Source)

Creativity and exile

Taras Shevchenko was sent into exile in 1847 by the tsarist decree of Nicholas I for his participation in the Cyril and Methodius Society and for his anti-authoritarian activities. According to the decree, the poet was forbidden to write and draw. Of course, the artist ignored the ban. During his exile, he created about 20 stories, 120 poems, and 450 paintings. (Source)

In 1857, thanks to the efforts of his friends, Shevchenko was returned from exile and in 1858 he arrived in Moscow, then in St. Petersburg. In 1859 he came to Ukraine, but was under police surveillance. He was forbidden to reside permanently in Ukraine and ordered to move to the capital. Ten years of exile and illness led to Taras Shevchenko's premature death on February 26, 1861. He was first buried at the Smolensk Cemetery in St. Petersburg, and in May 1861 his ashes were moved to Chernecha Hill near Kaniv. (Source)

Taras Shevchenko began writing poetry in the second half of the 1930s. In 1840, his first collection of poems “Kobzar” was published in St. Petersburg, and in 1845, the heroic romantic poem “The Haidamaks” was published. In 1843-45, he wrote a cycle of poems “Three Summers” (the central work of which is “Dream”), the poem “Caucasus,” the message “To the Dead and the Living...”, the poems “Chyhyryn, Chyhyryn,” “The Great Cellar,” “Standing in the Village of Subotiv,” and others, in which he sharply opposed the social and national oppression of the Ukrainian people. During his imprisonment, he wrote poetry, which he later combined into the cycle “In the Casemate”. In 1854-1858, he wrote the novels The Musician, The Artist, The Unfortunate, The Captain, and The Twins in Russian. Shevchenko's last prose works are the story “A Walk with Pleasure and Not Without Morals” (1856-1858) and his diary entries “Journal”. In Ukraine in 1858 Taras Shevchenko wrote a number of high examples of intimate and landscape poetry. Several of Shevchenko's works of this period were published in the magazine “Osnova” and the almanac “Hata”. (Source)
